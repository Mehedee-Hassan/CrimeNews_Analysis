which would equip them well for leaving home and gaining independence as adults.\n\nWhen they looked at the teenagers' brain scans, the researchers found activity in two areas of the brain - the hippocampus and the striatum - whereas adults mainly used their striatum.\n\nThey said these connections between two important parts of the growing brain explained why they performed better.\n\nThe part played by the hippocampus to reinforce learning during adolescence had not been recognised before, the study in Neuron said.\n\nIt is thought to play a key role in the formation of powerful memories during an important stage in life.\n\nJuliet Davidow, a psychology researcher at Harvard University, said the findings could inspire new ways of teaching teenagers.\n\n\xe2\x80\x98If you frame something positively, it could be the case that adolescents will remember things about the learning experience better.\n\n\xe2\x80\x98In everyday life, they're paying attention to their environment in a way that is different from adults.\xe2\x80\x99\n\nThe researchers are now looking at what other situations or experiences activate this link between the striatum and hippocampus in teenage brains."
